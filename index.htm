Phylogenetically‑Mixed Architectures

A general‑audience introduction to evolutionary AI design

Big Picture

Animals don’t rely on one giant, monolithic brain. Instead, they combine many specialized modules: vision, movement, reflexes, planning—each shaped by evolution. This is summarized as the phylogenetic position of embodied cognition. Phylogenetically‑mixed architectures apply this idea to embodied computational agents: evolve many components, then mix and match the best ones into a flexible agent. Phylogenetically-derived lineages are branching events that denote evolved components. Such lineages are controlled by two fitness functions: a fitness function that conserves lineage properties, and a creative fitness function that rewards deviations from mean lineage behavior. This work was developed in conjuntion with Microsoft CoPilot, which generated code for the models, helped to construct the benchmarking datasets, and provided a basis for the visualizations.

What Is a Phylogenetically‑Mixed Architecture?

The components of phylogenetically-mized architectures are derived from different evolutionary lineages that are reticulated via a hypergraph. Instead of training one model, the system generates many candidates, evaluates them, and assembles the best into a larger architecture. This larger architecture is characterized by multiple types of hypergraph. In the paper, two types of hypergraphs are used: bipartite and compositional. The bipartite graph is derived from concepots and beahviors representing a classic Cognitive Science thought experiment. The compositional graph is derived from different configurations of parts.

How the Pipeline Works

  • Source: Reservoir networks and diffusion processes generate variation.
  • Generator: A GAN explores the space and proposes new designs.
  • Selector: Evolutionary algorithms choose the best candidates.
  • Assembler: Hypergraphs combine components into multi‑scale architectures.

Demonstration Examples

The paper illustrates the pipeline using simple shapes and Braitenberg Vehicles, showing how evolutionary trees and hypergraphs can organize and recombine traits.

Why It Matters

This approach supports more adaptable, embodied AI systems—agents that can integrate multiple sensory systems, motor strategies, and behavioral modules.
